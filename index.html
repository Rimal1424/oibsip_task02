Vikram Sarabhai

1919 - 1971

Father of the Indian Space Program

Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai, a pioneering Indian physicist and visionary, is fondly remembered as the Father of the Indian Space Program. Born on August 12, 1919, in Ahmedabad, Gujarat, Sarabhai's contributions have left an indelible mark on India's scientific landscape.

Sarabhai was educated at Cambridge University, where he earned a doctorate in cosmic ray physics. Returning to India, he recognized the potential for space technology in the nation's development and established the Physical Research Laboratory (PRL) in Ahmedabad in 1947.

Under Sarabhai's leadership, the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) was formed in 1962, which eventually evolved into the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1969. He was instrumental in setting up the Thumba Equatorial Rocket Launching Station (TERLS) in Kerala and initiating India's first satellite program.

Sarabhai's vision extended beyond space exploration; he was a strong advocate for using technology to address societal issues. He initiated programs that led to the establishment of significant institutions like the Indian Institute of Management Ahmedabad (IIMA), the Community Science Centre, and the Darpana Academy for Performing Arts.

Recognizing the need for self-reliance in technology, Sarabhai also played a crucial role in establishing industries that could support space and nuclear research. His collaboration with Dr. Homi Bhabha was instrumental in the development of India's nuclear power program, further cementing his role as a key figure in the nation's scientific advancement.

Sarabhai was deeply committed to science education and public awareness. He believed that scientific knowledge should not be confined to laboratories but should reach the common people. This philosophy led to the founding of institutions like the Nehru Foundation for Development and the Vikram Sarabhai Community Science Centre, aimed at promoting science education at the grassroots level.

Sarabhai's influence extended internationally as well. He was a key figure in several global scientific and space organizations, and his work earned him international recognition. His leadership in the International Astronautical Federation and contributions to UNESCO underscored his global impact on science and technology policy.

Tragically, Sarabhai passed away on December 30, 1971, but his legacy continues to inspire generations of scientists and innovators. His life is a testament to the power of vision and determination in transforming a nation's capabilities. The Indian space program, with its numerous achievements including the Mars Orbiter Mission and Chandrayaan missions, stands as a tribute to his enduring legacy. -->

National Awards

  • Padma Vibhushan (posthumously in 1972)
  • Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar Award for Physics (1962)
  • Fellow of the Indian Academy of Sciences
  • Honorary doctorates from several universities worldwide.
